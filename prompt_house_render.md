## ARCHITECTURAL IDENTITY

This house is PARAGUAYAN FIRST. It draws from Guaraní and Spanish colonial traditions
translated into cob/earthen form. It does not look like an Earthship, a Hobbit hole,
a generic "mud house," or any foreign vernacular. It looks like something that grew
from Paraguay's red soil, built by someone who loves this specific land.

Core architectural language:
- Organic curved cob walls — no right angles anywhere in the entire structure
- White lime-washed exterior (warm white, not pure white — aged and breathable)
- Embedded bottle walls catching and transmitting coloured light
- Low-pitched living sod roof, wide overhangs on all sides
- Covered gallery (corredor) on the south-southeast face — toward the glade
- Interior courtyard depression between the two arms of the U-plan
- Tatakuá dome oven on the exterior south face
- Foundation stone matching the colonial terrace walls exactly — continuous material language
- Solar array on a separate steel frame to the east, NOT on the roof
- Micro-hydro turbine house visible in the distance at the weir (Zone 3)

## SITE PLACEMENT AND ORIENTATION

The house sits on the upper stone terrace platform.
The colonial terrace walls serve as the foundation retaining walls.
The house grows FROM the existing stone — not placed ON top of it.

ORIENTATION (Southern Hemisphere — critical):
- Gallery / corredor: faces SOUTH-SOUTHEAST — toward the glade, stream, and views
- Rear wall: faces NORTH-NORTHWEST — toward the escarpment and cool air source
- This means the gallery face is on the SHADED, COOL side (away from the equator)
- The tatakuá is on the NORTH-NORTHWEST exterior (rear/shaded face)
- The bottle walls are on the SOUTH-SOUTHEAST gallery face — they catch afternoon sun
  coming from the north-northwest and transmit coloured light INTO the gallery interior

HOW THE GOLDEN HOUR LIGHT WORKS (Variant A):
Sun comes from NORTH-NORTHWEST at 20° elevation (Southern Hemisphere afternoon)
→ Light passes through the bottle walls on the gallery face from OUTSIDE
→ Coloured light circles and ovals are cast on the gallery floor and rear wall inside
→ The viewer on the flat-rock pool (looking uphill/NNW) sees the lit gallery face
→ The whole structure appears to glow with coloured light at golden hour

FOOTPRINT:
U-shaped (horseshoe), opening toward the SOUTH-SOUTHEAST
Two long arms extending toward the glade and stream view
Rear joining wall partially buried in the hillside (bermed to the north)
The U-shape creates the interior courtyard between the arms
Overall footprint: approximately 18m wide × 14m deep
The house emerges from and continues the colonial stone terrace language below it

## WALLS — COB AND BOTTLE

### Cob wall specifications

Material: Paraguay red laterite clay + sand + straw (sourced from this property's soil)
Form: hand-sculpted, clearly formed by human hands — like a living sculpture
     No sharp corners anywhere. No flat planes. All surfaces gently curved.
     Walls thicken at base (80cm), narrow slightly toward top (50cm)
     Wall height: 2.8–3.2m, varying slightly
Evidence of craft: visible at close inspection —
     Finger marks and palm prints at human-scale heights
     Tool strokes in the plaster
     Slight bulges and organic variation in wall faces

Exterior finish layers (all visible at different exposure levels):
1. Rough cob (visible where lime has eroded at the very base, above the stone foundation)
2. Earth plaster (smooth, slightly textured, buff-orange where exposed)
3. Lime plaster (smoother, very slight orange-peel micro-texture)
4. Lime wash (outermost — white, slightly chalky, brush strokes visible up close)

Colour notes:
- Primary: warm white #F0EAD8 (lime wash — NOT pure white, slightly warm)
- Worn areas (base of walls, corner exposure): clay shows through #C4522A
- Recent repair patches: slightly brighter/whiter #F8F4EC
- Shaded north/northwest corners: faint grey-green algae beginning #C8C8B4
- The wall is NOT uniformly white — it has history and texture

### Bottle wall sections

PRIMARY LOCATION: The gallery-facing wall (south-southeast face) — the main visual feature.
SECONDARY LOCATIONS: Interior non-structural partition wall between gallery and main room.
ACCENT: One small section on the tatakuá side wall.

Bottle arrangement: organic clusters only — NOT grid patterns, NOT uniform rows
Two or three distinct cluster groupings on the gallery wall (30–50 bottles per cluster)
Bottles set mouth-to-mouth (openings inward), bottoms facing outward
Bottoms protrude 2–3cm from the lime plaster surface

Bottle colour mix within each cluster:
- Cobalt blue glass: #0047AB tinted — translucent cold blue
- Amber glass: #8B6914 tinted — warm orange-gold
- Green glass: #2D5A1B tinted — deep forest green
- Brown glass: #5C3A1A tinted — dark warm amber-brown
No two adjacent bottles the same colour. Organic randomness within each cluster.

Lime mortar between the bottles: aged texture, #E8DFC0, slightly different from wall lime

GOLDEN HOUR LIGHT EFFECT (Variant A — the most important visual moment):
The sun from NNW passes through the bottles on the gallery face
→ Coloured light patches cast on the gallery floor: cobalt circles, amber ovals, 
  green diamonds, brown ellipses
→ The patterns shift and overlap across the terracotta tile floor
→ The interior rear wall also catches these coloured spots
→ This is the cinematic signature of the house — make it beautiful

## KEY SHADER SETUPS

### Cob wall shader

Approach: two-layer material

Layer 1 — Clay body (visible where lime has worn):
```
Texture Coordinate → Mapping (scale 0.8)
  → Musgrave (Detail 6, Dimension 1.5, Lacunarity 2.0)  [large organic lumps]
  → Voronoi (Scale 8, F1)                               [surface grain variation]
  → Mix RGB (Multiply, Factor 0.3)
  → ColorRamp (#C4522A dark → #D8785A mid → #E8A870 highlight)
  → Principled BSDF (Roughness 0.85, SubsurfaceRadius 0.02)
  → Normal: combined displacement (0.3 strength)
```

Layer 2 — Lime wash (over-layer):
```
Colour: #F0EAD8 (warm white)
Roughness: 0.9 (matte, slightly chalky)
Normal: brush stroke direction noise at fine scale (0.05 strength)
Mix with clay layer: driven by AO map + wear mask (edges more exposed)
```

Fingerprint detail:
```
Fine Voronoi noise (Scale 60) mixed into Normal map
Very subtle — visible only at <50cm from surface
This is what makes the wall feel human-made
```

### Bottle glass shader

```
Principled BSDF:
  Base Color: bottle tint (see table above)
  Transmission: 1.0
  Roughness: 0.02
  IOR: 1.52
  Specular: 0.5
```

Enable Caustics in Cycles render settings.
The bottle caustics projected onto the gallery floor in Variant A are one of the
key beauty elements of the house render. Do not disable caustics to save render time.

### Lapacho timber shader (roof rafters, window frames, doors)

```
Principled BSDF:
  Base Color: #4A2D1A 
  Roughness: 0.55 (smooth but not polished)
  Sheen: 0.1 (slight wood sheen)
  Normal: subtle longitudinal grain texture (0.15 strength)
  For doors/frames: add horizontal grain variation in colour (#3A2010 darker veins)
```

### Iron / metal (window grilles, footbridge, solar frame)

Footbridge: dark #3A3A35, slight orange rust patches #8B4A1A at base and joints
Window grilles: same dark iron, rust at sharp edges and joints
Solar frame: galvanised #9A9A9A, very slight oxidation at bolt holes

## LIGHTING SETUPS

### Variant A — Golden Hour (Primary)

Sun direction: NORTH-NORTHWEST (azimuth ~330°)
Sun elevation: 20°
Colour: #F4A64A
Intensity: 3.5

HDRI: warm late-afternoon sky, partial cloud

Key lighting effects for the house:
1. Bottle wall illuminated from outside — sun passes through the south-southeast gallery 
   face bottles, projecting coloured light patches onto the gallery floor and rear wall
   Model this explicitly — the floor tile shows cobalt, amber, green, brown discs
2. The lapacho timber soffit catches warm raking light from below the overhang
3. The tatakuá dome is in shadow (rear face, north-northwest) — only the opening glows 
   orange from the fire inside (add a small orange point light inside)
4. The stone foundation catches strong raking light — the moss and quartzite texture pops
5. Small area light inside the gallery (warm #F4A030, intensity 0.3) — simulates 
   reflected warm light from the tile floor

### Variant B — Overcast Dawn

No directional sun

### Variant C — Night

Dark exterior: deep blue #0A1A2A sky, slight star field
Interior lighting (warm) through:
  - Windows: warm golden light #F4A030 spilling from interior
  - Bottle walls: each bottle glows individually from inside — coloured constellation 
    effect on the exterior face (cobalt, amber, green, brown points of light)
  - Gallery lamp: one hanging terracotta-shaded lamp, warm #F4A030
  - Tatakuá dome: orange glow at the opening from the fire inside
The property feels warmly inhabited against the dark forest and night sky

## WHAT TO EXCLUDE

- Any right angles in the cob walls (a right angle is a modelling error — fix it)
- Cement plaster on any clay wall (always lime)
- Any standing water on the property without flow-through
- Power poles or grid infrastructure
- Solar panels on the living roof (separate frame to the east only)
- Any coconut palm assets (the pindo palm requires custom modelling)
- Uniform or manufactured-looking plants (this is a working farm, not a resort)
- Any generic "mud house" or fantasy hobbit aesthetic
- Clean, new, or unweathered stone or metal
- Blue or clear stream water (it is red-brown below Zone 1)
- Any symmetry or uniformity in the bottle wall placement

## THE SCENE NARRATIVE

When someone looks at this render, the story it tells must be:

"Someone who loves this land built a home from it. The clay came from under their feet.
The bottles were emptied by people who gathered here. The timber was from the forest
that now shades the roof. The stones in the foundation are the same ones in the 
hundred-year-old walls below. The stream that passes the gallery has run past this spot
forever — before the house, and long after. The pink petals on the path fell this morning
from the lapacho that was here long before anyone built anything. The tatakuá is warm.
Someone is home."

Every material choice, plant placement, camera angle, and light decision serves this story.
