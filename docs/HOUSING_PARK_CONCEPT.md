## 2. Concept models — eight directions

For each: what it is, who it serves, why it fits this site, what to watch out for.

### 2.1 Boutique eco-retreat
A small (8–16 key) high-end hospitality property with a central restaurant, spa, and 1–2 pools. Weekly stays, all-inclusive pricing, low guest count, high per-guest spend.
- **Best for:** Asunción weekenders + Argentine/Brazilian winter tourists (Iguazú-bound flow)
- **Why it fits:** the stream, forest, and quiet are luxury products here; cob/earthen architecture is on-brand for the eco positioning
- **Watch:** operational complexity (staff, food, laundry, waste), seasonality (peak Jun–Aug, low Nov–Feb), capex per key ~$30–80k

### 2.2 Agrotourism estate
Working farm (vegetables, fruit, herbs, chickens, possibly cattle) with a farm-to-table restaurant and 4–8 guest rooms/cabins. Guests can participate in farm activities.
- **Best for:** culinary travelers, families, school groups, agronomists
- **Why it fits:** red laterite is genuinely productive, the terraces are pre-farmed, Ita Cajón's flow brings curious visitors already
- **Watch:** farm operation ≠ hospitality operation; needs two distinct skill sets; food licensing is non-trivial in Paraguay

### 2.3 Sustainable co-housing community
5–15 private family homes (sold or long-leased) sharing a common house, restaurant/café, gardens, pool, workshop, and possibly agricultural plots. Members share governance and maintenance.
- **Best for:** like-minded families wanting rural life with reduced isolation; expat community
- **Why it fits:** the site's 62 ha supports ~10–20 households at low density without disturbing forest
- **Watch:** legal form is complex (paraguayan "consorcio"? cooperative? HOA-equivalent?), need clear property rights + shared infrastructure governance, social dynamics can fracture

### 2.4 Hospitality resort with event space
20–40 keys, full restaurant, dedicated event hall (200+ pax) for weddings, corporate retreats, and family celebrations. Year-round weekend + seasonal event business.
- **Best for:** Asunción's middle/upper class for weddings; corporate offsite market
- **Why it fits:** Paraguarí is within reach of Asunción's event market; the existing cob house could be the bridal suite or a premium cabin
- **Watch:** events business is high-touch, low-margin, weather-dependent in Oct–Apr rainy season; needs big parking + service road

### 2.5 Glamping / outdoor hospitality
6–20 luxury tents or platform cabins scattered through forest clearings, communal bathhouse, central restaurant. Low capital cost per key.
- **Best for:** nature-immersive travelers, weekend couples, social-media market
- **Why it fits:** Atlantic Forest + stream is the product; minimal ground disturbance
- **Watch:** tent durability in summer storms + humidity, septic for dispersed sites, security, insurance for high-value tents

### 2.6 Artist / creative residency
4–8 live-work units, shared studio, gallery, performance space, residencies 2–12 weeks. Restaurant optional but helpful.
- **Best for:** artists, writers, musicians, craftspeople; international residencies
- **Why it fits:** the quiet and natural beauty genuinely support creative work; paraguayan craft traditions (ñandutí, ao poí, karanday, ceramics) connect to broader regional art market
- **Watch:** residency business is grant-dependent; need a sponsor or anchor institution to be viable; very low revenue per unit

### 2.7 Educational / regenerative farm
Permaculture or regenerative-agriculture campus with 20–40 students in courses lasting days–weeks, dormitory-style lodging, communal kitchen.
- **Best for:** agronomy students, permaculture practitioners, school groups, international volunteers (WWOOF-style)
- **Why it fits:** Paraguay's farming culture is real, and there's a global demand for tropical-regeneration learning sites
- **Watch:** seasonality of courses, need for a strong program director, food licensing, accommodation code for dormitories

### 2.8 Multi-family compound
Wesley's primary home + 3–6 guest/family houses on shared land, with shared restaurant/kitchen, pool, and grounds. Used privately and for visiting family/friends.
- **Best for:** a personal estate rather than commercial
- **Why it fits:** the 62 ha gives room; the existing cob house could be Wesley's house, additional houses for guests
- **Watch:** commercial activity may be limited by land classification (see §6.1); ongoing staff costs are real; doesn't generate revenue

### 2.9 Hybrid (most likely outcome)
Real projects often combine 2–3 of the above. Common pairings: **co-housing + retreat** (private owners + paying guests), **agrotourism + restaurant** (farm supplies restaurant), **resort + events** (weekend hospitality + wedding business). Worth thinking about which combinations are mutually reinforcing.

## 4. Restaurant — depth

The restaurant is the most concrete and possibly most valuable single component. A few angles:

### 4.1 Format
- **Quincho abierto** (open-air BBQ pavilion, Paraguayan standard) — low capex, suits asado/parrilla, very on-brand
- **Indoor formal dining** — for evening service, climate-controlled
- **Poolside casual** — lighter menu, drinks, daytime
- **Multi-zone** — quincho by day, formal room by night, pool bar at sunset

### 4.2 Cuisine direction
- **Asado / parrilla** — Paraguayan BBQ, lower complexity, traditional, weekend institution
- **Traditional Paraguaya** — chipá, sopa paraguaya, surubí, mbejú, mbocayá, kosereña — culturally distinctive, low competitor density
- **Farm-to-table** — depends on the agrotourism angle; high appeal, supply-chain complexity
- **Café / panadería** — chipá + coffee + cocido, daytime-only, simple operation
- **International** — pasta, pizza, sushi; high competition, less differentiation
- **Hybrid** — asado Sun, café mornings, fine-dining weekends

### 4.3 Capacity
- 30 covers is a manageable small operation
- 50–60 covers is a regional destination scale
- 80+ covers needs serious kitchen + staff

### 4.4 Seasonality
Paraguayan hospitality is **inversely seasonal** to the Northern Hemisphere: high season is **June–August** (Asunción residents escaping the city winter cold, plus Argentines/Brazilians visiting). Low season is **Nov–Feb** (Asunción residents leave for vacation abroad, and rural Paraguay is brutally hot). Plan for ~60% revenue in 4 winter months.

### 4.5 Business models
- **Owner-operator**: full control, high time commitment
- **Leased to a chef/operator**: less involvement, but harder to control brand
- **Franchise / chain partner**: possible but not very Paraguayan
- **Cooperative with a known local restaurant brand**: well-trodden in Paraguay

### 4.6 Regulatory
- Bromatología (health) inspection + permit
- Bomberos (fire) inspection
- Municipal habilitación as commercial establishment
- RUC + Timbrado for invoicing
- Seguro de responsabilidad civil
- Alcohol license if serving (separate)

### 4.7 Supply chain considerations
- **Produce**: own farm + nearby Escobar producers
- **Meat**: nearby frigorífico (Frigomerc, Mussa, local carnicerías)
- **Fish**: river fish (surubí, pacú) from the Paraguay/Paraná basins, not the local stream
- **Specialty imports**: harder in Paraguay, customs friction, factor in 4–6 week lead times for non-staples
- **Firewood**: if serving asado, need sustainable source (own forest, otherwise illegal logging issue)

## 6. Paraguay-specific considerations

This is the section that has to be right because it's what AI Whisperers' local knowledge brings to the table.

### 6.1 Legal / land use
- **Land classification**: the 62 ha is currently classified as rural (finca rural). Commercial activities (restaurant, hotel) may require **reclassification** or a commercial-use permit. This is handled at the municipal level (Municipalidad de Escobar). It's not impossible but it's a real process.
- **Anexo I of the boleto** (technical descriptions of each finca) — see `docs/contract_summary.md`. This defines what can be built where within the unified property.
- **Subdivisión**: if Wesley sells plots (co-housing model), each lot needs to be subdivided and escriturado separately, with municipal subdivision approval.
- **Servidumbres de paso**: any access road crossing neighboring fincas needs an established easement — usually done at escritura signing or by separate escritura de servidumbre.
- **Forest protection**: Atlantic Forest remnants are subject to **Ley 422/73 (Forestal)** and **Ley 294/93 (Evaluación de impacto ambiental)**. Cutting mature trees may require permission from INFONA (Instituto Forestal Nacional) and/or MADES (Ministerio del Ambiente). The 50–100 year old terraces have heritage value worth preserving.
- **Stream protection**: the spring-fed stream likely falls under **INAA (Instituto Nacional del Agua)** jurisdiction. Any water extraction, dam modification (the existing weir), or building within the riparian zone may need permits.

### 6.2 Tax / financial
- **RUC** (tax ID) — required for any business. Get one for the operating entity before opening.
- **Timbrado** — every invoice/receipt needs a timbrado number from SET (Subsecretaría de Estado de Tributación). Renew annually.
- **IVA** — 10% on services, 5% on some basics. Restaurants typically charge 10% on meals.
- **IRE / IRP** — corporate or personal income tax. The business structure (S.A. vs S.R.L. vs E.A.S. vs personal) drives which applies.
- **IMAGRO** — rural real estate tax, low but payable.
- **Permiso municipal** — annual business permit from the Municipalidad.
- **Bancard / Pagopar** — for card payments; most restaurants take both. Avoid assuming Stripe (no Paraguayan onboarding).
- **Banco**: Itaú, Ueno, or Banco Familiar for B2B banking; account needs both PYG and USD.
- **Billeteras móviles**: Tigo Money, Personal Pay for staff payments and customer tips.
- **Foreign-currency income**: if Wesley and Thijs are Dutch and remitting, factor in transfer costs + the PYG/USD volatility.

### 6.3 Cultural
- **Bilingual**: Spanish + Guaraní. Signage, menus, staff should ideally be bilingual. The most distinctive brand move is **trilingual** (Guaraní/Spanish/English) for the international tourism market.
- **Asunción elite weekend pattern**: Friday evening arrival, Sunday afternoon departure. Restaurants and hotels should target this rhythm first.
- **Sunday family lunch** is sacred. A Sunday asado can become the signature offer.
- **Tereré / mate culture**: providing good tereré service (cold yerba, cold water, good bombilla) is a strong cultural cue.
- **Music**: polca paraguaya and guarania as ambient music is a 100% Paraguayan brand cue. Live folklore music on weekends is a draw.
- **Hospitality warmth**: Paraguayans are generally warm hosts. Staff training should reinforce this rather than impose international corporate hospitality norms.
- **Machismo legacy**: women traveling alone, with kids, or in female groups need to feel safe and welcomed. Design and policies should explicitly counter this.
- **Religious**: Catholic heritage (chapel possible), but evangelical Christianity is growing. Inclusive design matters.
- **Indigenous communities**: the Mbopicua name suggests a Guaraní historical reference; respectful acknowledgement of indigenous heritage is appropriate but should be researched and consulted, not appropriated.

### 6.4 Climate / dengue
- **Dengue protocol is non-negotiable**: no standing water anywhere. Pool must be filtered and chlorinated. Cisterns must be sealed with 0.5 mm mesh. Window screens on all lodging.
- **Summer storms** (Oct–Apr) can be violent: lightning, microbursts, 100mm+ in a few hours. Buildings need to be storm-rated; outdoor furniture needs to be tied down or stored.
- **Heat mitigation**: passive design first (corredor, cross-ventilation, deep overhangs, thermal mass, ceiling fans). AC as backup for lodging only. The 10 design rules in `MASTER_BRIEF.md` are already aligned with this.
- **Humidity + cob**: cob walls need lime plaster (not cement), wide overhangs (90cm+), raised foundation (60cm+), all per the existing rules.
- **Flooding**: the lower stream zones may flood. Buildings should be on the upper terraces, not in the floodplain.
- **Power outages**: ANDE is unreliable in rural Paraguay. Micro-hydro + solar + LiFePO4 (already in the design) is the right answer. Commercial kitchens may need generator backup for refrigeration.

### 6.5 Environmental / forest
- **Atlantic Forest is irreplaceable**: 92% of Paraguay's original Atlantic Forest has been cleared. This site is part of the remaining 8%. Clearing for development should be minimized.
- **Mature trees must be preserved**: each one is decades old. Building footprints should thread between existing trees.
- **Invasive species**: bamboo clumping is good (Guadua/Chusquea native); running bamboo is bad. Agave is fine but spreads. Lantana, eucalyptus, and pinus are invasive and should be avoided.
- **Wildlife corridor**: the forest likely connects to other fragments. Maintaining canopy continuity matters for fauna.
- **Water source protection**: the stream is the source. Septic systems, agricultural runoff, and animal waste must be kept out of the watershed.

### 6.6 Tourism patterns
- **Domestic tourism**: Asunción residents are the main market. They want nature, fresh air, traditional food, and a short drive.
- **Argentinian / Brazilian tourism**: significant flow to eastern Paraguay, especially in Mercosur school holidays (July, January-February, Easter).
- **Iguazú transit**: tourists heading to/from Iguazú sometimes stop in eastern Paraguay. Targeting this requires being on their route.
- **International ecotourism**: small but premium market. Needs strong web presence, English-speaking staff, and recognizable certifications (sustainability credentials, regenerative practices).
- **Paraguarí specifically**: known for cerros (Cerro Perõ, Cerro Hu), the Ybycuí National Park, and religious tourism (Caacupé is in the next department). Escobar itself is a small agricultural town with limited existing tourism.

### 6.7 Staff / labor
- **Local hiring**: Escobar and Paraguarí are the immediate labor pool. Service staff, kitchen help, maintenance, guides.
- **Specialist hiring**: chef, agronomist, marketing manager likely come from Asunción. Weekly commute or on-site housing needed.
- **Transport**: many staff won't have cars. Site is rural. Bus service from Paraguarí to Escobar is limited. Wesley likely needs to provide staff transport.
- **Training**: hospitality training in Paraguay is thin. AI Whisperers can curate or create internal training material.
- **Legal**: full-time staff need formal employment (IPS, aportes), not informal arrangements. Factor into the cost model.

### 6.8 Construction / supply
- **Cob / earthen**: very cheap locally (clay is free, sand is free, labor is local). Needs skilled hands; we have the design research already.
- **Timber (lapacho, cedro)**: high quality, but lapacho is protected; cedro is increasingly scarce. Replanting and FSC sourcing matter.
- **Bamboo (Guadua)**: native, fast-growing, strong, but needs treatment against insects.
- **Imported materials**: kitchen equipment, AC units, pool equipment — customs + logistics add 4–6 weeks. Order early.
- **Skilled labor**: cob artisans, timber framers, pool builders, electricians — sourcing in rural Paraguay is hit-and-miss. AI Whisperers can help vet.
- **Permit timelines**: municipal permits for rural construction are usually fast (weeks) but commercial permits can take months. Plan ahead.
