# Cultural Notes — La Quebrada Viva (Escobar, Paraguarí)

**Status:** living document. Adds verifiable cultural design constraints to back Rule 8 ("culturally Paraguayan first"). Refer here when in doubt whether a design choice reads as authentic Paraguayan or imported pastiche.

## Why this exists

Wesley's audience for the housing park is European / 1st-world travelers. The temptation is to design something "exotic-tropical" — Bali pavilion, Tuscan villa, Earthship dome — because that is easier to picture. Rule 8 forbids that. This file captures the specific cultural cues that make the result feel **of Paraguay**, not lifted from Pinterest.

## Core architectural vocabulary (use these, not their cosmopolitan substitutes)

| Use this | Not this | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Corredor** (wraparound covered verandah, 1.8–2.5 m deep) | Lanai, terrace, porch | The corredor is THE Paraguayan outdoor room. It mediates heat, rain, and social life. Indispensable for Rule 6 + 8. |
| **Tatakuá** (wood-fired clay oven, dome) | Pizza oven, tandoor | The tatakuá is the kitchen of the campo. Chipa, mbeju, sopa paraguaya, asados — all of it routes through this dome. Place it OUTSIDE the kitchen, in a courtyard or under a roofed extension of the corredor. |
| **Patio interno** (central court) | Atrium, courtyard | Inherited from Spanish colonial; works for cross-ventilation in the 35 °C summers. |
| **Low-pitched sod roof / red tile** | Steep gable, thatched A-frame, flat modernist roof | Low-pitch (15–25°) survives the Paraguayan rain pattern (short, intense). |
| **Lapacho timber** | Imported teak, ipê (the Brazilian export name for lapacho), bamboo for structure | Lapacho is the national tree. Locally sourced, FSC available. |
| **Ñandutí lace as decorative motif** | Generic "ethnic textile" | Itauguá-made; the regional craft for which Paraguay is known. |
| **Aó po'í embroidered cotton** | Generic linen | Used for cushions, runners, light curtains. |
| **Hammock** | Outdoor lounge chair | The hammock IS the corredor furniture. |
| **Mate / tereré set on a low table** | Coffee table service | The mate/tereré ritual is the daily social glue. A guampa + bombilla + termo signals "this is the social spot". |

## Materials and colors authoritative for Paraguay (not Mediterranean / Asian / Northern European)

- **Cob (adobe)**: red-tan to ochre, NEVER bleached white. Lime-wash to soft cream or honey, not pure white.
- **Roof tile**: terracotta, weathered. Or grass sod in the LQV variant.
- **Floor inside**: polished red brick OR red-laterite-tinted concrete (NOT cement-grey).
- **Floor on corredor**: rough-laid sandstone or red brick.
- **Doors**: solid lapacho or urunday, with iron hardware (not bronze, not brass).
- **Window frames**: lapacho with simple square mullions; no Mediterranean shutters, no Asian shoji.
- **Lighting fixtures**: hand-blown glass, terracotta, or wrought iron — never anodized aluminum, never brushed nickel, never bare Edison-bulb hipster minimalism.

## Plant language (Atlantic Forest, NOT generic tropical)

- **Lapacho** (*Handroanthus impetiginosus*) — the national tree, deciduous, hot-pink trumpet flowers in winter. Defines Variant A.
- **Pindo palm** (*Syagrus romanzoffiana*) — plumose drooping fronds. NOT coconut, NOT date palm. The campo palm.
- **Mango** — huge round canopy, the shade tree of every rural farmhouse.
- **Mburucuyá** (passionfruit) — climber for pergolas. Edible.
- **Yerba mate** (*Ilex paraguariensis*) — the cultural beverage; grown as understory shrub. Plant a few near the corredor as a cultural cue.
- **Sugar cane** (caña dulce) — interpretive crop plot; signals campo farming.
- **Maize and mandioca** — the staple subsistence pair. Visible in the kitchen garden.
- **Ka'a he'ẽ (stevia)** — native sweetener; a Paraguayan botanical signature.

Avoid: bird-of-paradise, plumeria, heliconia (Costa Rica), bougainvillea-as-headliner (Mediterranean coding), frangipani (Bali coding), eucalyptus (Australian coding, invasive in Paraguay).

## Food & hospitality signaling

Even though we are visualizing, not catering, the renders read more authentic when the food and ritual props match.

- A chipa cooling rack on the corredor table.
- A tereré set: cuernos (horn cup) + bombilla + termo with cold water + a small pot of yuyos (mint, menta, perejil).
- Sopa paraguaya pan on the tatakuá (it's a corn-cake, not a soup, and is unmistakably Paraguayan).
- A side of grilled provoleta if hosting an asado (Argentine influence; common in Paraguay).
- Honey from local meliponas (stingless bees) in a clay jar — a Wesley-aligned eco signal.

## Music & sound cues (for V/O and video later; not strictly relevant to stills, but for narrative consistency)

- Polka paraguaya and guarania (Agustín Pío Barrios "Mangoré").
- Harp arpa paraguaya — solo or as background.
- No Buena Vista Social Club (Cuba), no bossa nova (Brazil), no son cubano. They are wrong-country tells.

## Religious / spiritual signaling

- Small altar with a Virgin of Caacupé image (Paraguay's patroness) on the corredor or kitchen wall is normal and not preachy.
- Cross at the property entry on a wooden post — traditional rural marker.
- Do NOT depict Buddha statues, OM symbols, Hindu deities, Catholic Italian-style frescoes, or generic Latin American "saints'-day" markets.

## Language signaling

- Paraguay is officially bilingual: **Spanish + Guaraní**. Signage anywhere visible in the renders should be in BOTH (e.g., a wood-burned plaque: "TATAKUÁ" / "Cocina Antigua").
- "Quebrada Viva" itself works because it's standard Spanish for "living ravine". A Guaraní co-name like **"Yvyra'í Reñói"** ("the saplings sprout") may be added on a plaque later.
- Avoid Portuguese (that signals Brazil). Avoid pure English signage; it would read as either gringo-resort or AirBnb-faceless.

## What an experienced Paraguayan visitor should notice in the renders

1. The **corredor is generous** (>1.8 m), not a token strip. It has a hammock, not lounge chairs.
2. The **tatakuá is outside** the kitchen and on the corredor's social side.
3. The **roof overhang** is wide enough to keep the corredor dry in a tormenta (>90 cm).
4. The **windows are framed in lapacho** with simple mullions.
5. **Mate/tereré ritual** is staged somewhere.
6. **Lapacho tree** is the hero plant. Pindo palms are background.
7. **No coconut palms anywhere.**
8. **Bottles in the wall** read as a craft expression, not as kitsch — embedded flush, often pointing into the room so the morning light passes through.

## What a Paraguayan would call out as wrong

1. Coconut palms or banana trees as primary scenography (those are coastal-Brazilian / Caribbean tells).
2. Stucco-white-wall + blue-shutter combo (Greek tell).
3. Bali stone heads, lava rock walls (Indonesian tell).
4. Bonfire pit in a metal ring (American backyard tell — should be the tatakuá or a simple stone ring).
5. Imported decking that isn't lapacho or urunday.
6. Generic "rainforest" lush dense undergrowth — the Atlantic Forest in the lower-density Paraguarí zone is mixed: some patches dense, some clearings, some agro-forestry corridors.
